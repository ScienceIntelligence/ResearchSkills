## Situation
A researcher proposed a new named "law" (analogous to Moore's Law) for a domain-specific review paper. The initial draft articulated the concept in qualitative, narrative terms without equations, quantitative thresholds, or cited empirical evidence.

## Action
The draft was accepted and written up as a standalone conceptual note — essentially a slogan dressed as a scientific principle, with no testable criterion attached.

## Outcome
The researcher immediately rejected the draft as non-scientific: "sci facts can be backed or antied using ground truth." The note was rewritten from scratch around a falsifiable quantitative criterion, explicit equations, and empirical evidence from the literature that could support or contradict the claim.

## Lesson
A named scientific law or principle is not scientific until it has: (1) a precise quantitative statement (equation or threshold), and (2) at least one published datum that could falsify it. Narrative plausibility is not a substitute. When proposing any new empirical regularity in a review paper, ground it in equations and cite contradictory evidence alongside supporting evidence before presenting it.

## Retrieval Cues
- Proposing a new "law," "principle," or "scaling relation" in a review
- First draft of a hypothesis contains no equations
- Conceptual framing without cited empirical anchors
- Reviewer or collaborator says the claim is "not scientific"
