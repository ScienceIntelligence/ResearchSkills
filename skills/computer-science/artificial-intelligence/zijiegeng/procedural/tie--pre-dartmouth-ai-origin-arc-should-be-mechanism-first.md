## When + Exclusions
When structuring a chapter on AI before the Dartmouth conference.

Exclude a narrow chapter only about the Dartmouth meeting itself.

## Decision
Preferred: organize the pre-Dartmouth origin story around mechanisms that made AI thinkable: formal neuron, stored-program computer, information/control/systems theory, early game and theorem programs, then Dartmouth as disciplinary crystallization.

Rejected: jumping directly from Turing to Dartmouth.  
Rejected: treating Dartmouth as the beginning of the ideas rather than the naming and institutionalization of the field.

Reasoning: the richer research narrative is that AI emerged when several technical-philosophical streams made “intelligence as mechanism” plausible.

## Local Verifiers
- McCulloch-Pitts-style neuron formalization appears before connectionism.
- Stored-program architecture explains why programs became manipulable objects.
- Information theory, cybernetics, and systems theory are treated as different lenses.
- Early checkers/theorem-proving programs bridge theory and demonstration.
- Dartmouth is framed as disciplinary birth, not conceptual ex nihilo.

## Failure Handling
If the chapter feels scattered, use one recurring question: “How did intelligence become formalizable, computable, and engineerable?”

If the chapter feels too technical, anchor each mechanism in a concrete historical scene.

## Anti-exemplars
- “AI began in 1956.”
- A chapter that treats neural networks, computers, and cybernetics as unrelated anecdotes.
