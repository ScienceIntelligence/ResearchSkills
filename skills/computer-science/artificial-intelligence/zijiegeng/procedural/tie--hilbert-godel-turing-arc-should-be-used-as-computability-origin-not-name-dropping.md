## When + Exclusions
When explaining the philosophical and mathematical origins of AI through formal logic and computability.

Exclude purely biographical accounts of Hilbert, Gödel, or Turing.

## Decision
Preferred: connect Hilbert's program, Gödel's incompleteness, and Turing's computability as a single arc about the limits and mechanization of formal reasoning.

Rejected: treating these figures as independent historical milestones.  
Rejected: jumping from computability to modern AI without explaining the conceptual transformation.

Reasoning: AI becomes intellectually possible when reasoning is formalized, bounded, and mechanized. The arc is about what can be made mechanical and what cannot.

## Local Verifiers
- Hilbert supplies the dream of formal completeness.
- Gödel exposes internal limits.
- Turing turns formal procedure into machine computation.
- The chapter explains how this prepares the ground for AI.

## Failure Handling
If the section feels like mathematical exposition, reframe around the question: “Can thought be reduced to rule-following?”

If it feels too philosophical, anchor it in formal systems and computability.

## Anti-exemplars
- “Gödel proved machines cannot think” as a simplistic takeaway.
- “Turing invented computers, then AI happened.”
